01 / 03 Fintech · Compliance SaaS

B2B SaaS platform automating KYB onboarding and fraud detection for financial services teams

Regulon

What if compliance onboarding felt as clear as anything else people sign up for?

Executive Summary

Regulon is a B2B compliance platform for financial services teams, and I led the redesign of its AML/KYC onboarding flow, the point where its most operationally important users most often gave up. The original flow used a step-by-step wizard that hid the scope of what compliance required until users were already inside it. Testing showed that hidden scope, not the compliance requirement itself, was driving abandonment. I redesigned the flow to show the full onboarding journey upfront, let users move between sections instead of forcing linear progress, and rebuilt the underlying components into a reusable system.

02 / 03 Proptech · Web & Mobile

Proptech startup building property management software for real estate operators across Africa

Dormot

Four tools, zero coordination. Property operators in Africa needed one place that actually worked.

Role Product Designer Tools Figma, FigJam, Notion
Problem

African real estate operators were managing property listings, guest access, and short stays across four disconnected tools, with no single source of operational truth.

The constraint we had to name

Early designs tried to serve property operators and tenants equally from a shared interface. Mid-project we surfaced the tension: designing for two audiences simultaneously was diluting the experience for both. We made the explicit decision to prioritise operators as the primary user, tenants became a simpler, separate experience. It was a hard conversation but it unlocked the design.

Process
  • Designed onboarding and operational workflows spanning mobile and web in one product
  • Ran discovery workshops to surface real operator needs over internal assumptions
  • Maintained a scalable, documented design system to eliminate repetitive effort

03 / 03 Consumer · Food Delivery · AI

AI-assisted food ordering and discovery app launched in the Nigerian consumer market

Pot of Food

What if you could order exactly what you want, not just what the menu allows?

Role Product Designer Tools Figma, InVision, Miro
Problem

Existing food ordering apps locked users into rigid menus with no room to customise, a real barrier for communities with specific food cultures and preferences.

Direction we abandoned

We built discovery around cooks posting available dishes. Users kept trying to specify what they wanted before ordering. The content model was backwards. We flipped it two weeks before handoff.

Process
  • Designed an AI-assisted discovery and customisation flow for meal ordering
  • Built onboarding focused on accessibility, simplicity, and first-order retention
  • Iterated on ordering clarity using direct customer behaviour and feedback

About

Good design isn't
about making things
beautiful. It's about making
complex systems feel simple.

I'm a product designer, not a UI designer. I care less about how a screen looks and more about why a workflow is losing people, why a compliance form scares users off at step three, or why a dashboard exists that nobody on the team actually trusts.

Eight years in, mostly across SaaS, fintech, proptech, and healthcare, mostly on the unglamorous, expensive-to-get-wrong parts of a product. I work end to end, from the first messy conversation with a stakeholder through to developer handoff, and I use AI tools to move faster through the parts of that process that don't need my judgement.

Day-to-day
  • How I collaborateI'd rather sit in on a messy conversation early than get handed a clean brief late. Most of what ends up in a final design comes out of arguing through tradeoffs with the people who'll build and sell it, not from time alone in Figma.
  • How I work with engineersI bring engineers in while I'm still sketching, not once the design is finished. They catch constraints I'd miss on my own, and I'd rather change a flow at the whiteboard than after it's already been built.
  • How I work with Product ManagersI treat the roadmap as something we write together, not something handed to me. My job is to push back when a requirement is solving the wrong problem, and to bring evidence when I do, not just an opinion.
  • How I facilitate workshopsA workshop only earns its hour if it changes what someone in the room believes. I run them to get disagreement out in the open early, not to end up with a nice-looking board of stickers nobody acts on.
  • How I use researchI use research to find out where I'm wrong, not to confirm what I already planned to build. If a round of testing doesn't change anyone's plans, we probably tested the wrong thing.
  • Balancing user needs and business goalsThese two rarely conflict as much as people assume, they're just measured differently. I look for the version of a solution that makes someone's day easier and still moves the number the business is watching, and it's almost always there if you look hard enough.
